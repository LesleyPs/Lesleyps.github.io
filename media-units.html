With a single design change—adding a subtle bounce animation to a directional arrow—I With limited engineering resources, my initial task was a single, high-impact change. My solution was to add a subtle bounce animation to a single directional arrow, a small but critical visual cue that guided the user from tab to tab.
The success of this small change, which more than doubled the initial click-through rate, validated the importance of product design and led to a more robust, research-driven process. My design process was grounded in a blend of user testing, A/B and multivariate testing, and quantitative data analysis. This allowed me to gather qualitative insights from travelers, validate design hypotheses, and track user behavior to ensure every decision was data-driven.

I also designed a range of other products, from complex widgets and sleek Expedia-branded units to highly effective units as small as a button, all built on the same core principle of adding value.
To combat "banner blindness," I was tasked with transforming space traditionally used for static banners into useful, native-feeling tools that would enhance the user's media experience and drive performance.
I was able to initiate dedicated user research that challenged stakeholder assumptions. User testing showed that brand recognition and color drew users in, significantly increasing engagement, despite initial concerns that large logos would feel like banners. This key insight, combined with continuous A/B testing, allowed the design to evolve, transforming the Tab-unit into a high-performing product.
